Barberini Faun, late third – early second century BCE

Munich, Glyptothek

Marble

Located in College Hall

On loan from The Metropolitan Museum of Art

The statue represents a sleeping Faun, a mythical woodland creature that looks like a human with a tail and was a follower of Bacchus. Fauns were known in Classical times for wild drinking and uncontrolled carousing in the wild. The faun depicted here has dropped his pan-pipes behind the rock on which he lies asleep, splayed out on his clothing, a panther-skin with gaping jaws. The faun’s brow is knitted and his mouth is slightly open, suggesting that his drunken sleep is not very comfortable.
The marble statue of the Faun was dug up in Rome in the 1620’s, during Pope Urban VIII’s fortification of the Castel Sant’Angelo. The Castel was built originally as a Mausoleum for the Emperor Hadrian in the 2nd century BCE and decorated with numerous statues, including the Barberini Faun. Urban VIII’s family, the Barberini family, owned the statue after it was found. During the 17th and 18th centuries, the Barberini Faun became one of the most famous antique statues in Rome. Perhaps the fact that it was poorly preserved made it even more tantalizing for artists and connoisseurs. A number of different artists adjusted the statue’s posture, and restored and re-restored its missing parts, including the right leg, the left arm, the fingers of the right hand, and the base. The fig-leaf was added during the 1600’s.
During the French occupation of Italy, the members of the Barberini family were forced to sell the statue. Despite strenuous resistance to its export, the statue was sold to Ludwig I of Bavaria in 1814. It arrived in Munich in 1820, where a special room was built for it in the Glyptothek, where it remains today.
